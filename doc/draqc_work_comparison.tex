\subsection{A robust peer comparison with degree-aware aggregation}\label{sec:draqc}

The aggregation-based multigrid of Napov and Notay---degree-aware rooted aggregation
with quality control (DRA-QC) \cite{napov-notay}---is the graph-Laplacian
solver most closely related to LAMG\@. Like LAMG it eliminates low-degree vertices and
coarsens by aggregation; unlike LAMG it controls aggregate quality by a provable two-grid
condition-number bound rather than by an energy guard, and is reported $2$--$5\times$ faster
than the original LAMG on a $142$-graph real-world test set. As no implementation of the
DRA-QC variant is publicly distributed, we reimplemented it faithfully in the same language
(validated to reproduce the published operator complexities and $15$--$30$-iteration range on
that test set) and compare it to LAMG+ on identical operators.

\emph{Iteration counts alone are not a fair comparison}: a DRA-QC K-cycle iteration and a
LAMG+ $\gamma$-cycle differ in cost, because the two build hierarchies of very different
operator complexity (DRA-QC's lean aggregation gives $\mathcal C_A\approx1.0$--$1.4$; LAMG+'s
caliber-2 and $\gamma{=}1.5$ cycle give $1.2$--$3.3$). We therefore report
\emph{work per edge} (WPE)---the total operator work of a full solve normalized by the edge
count,
\[
\mathrm{WPE}\;=\;\frac{n_{\mathrm{it}}}{m}\sum_{\ell} v_\ell\bigl(p\cdot\mathrm{nnz}(A_\ell)+\mathrm{nnz}(P_\ell)+\mathrm{nnz}(R_\ell)\bigr),
\]
where $n_{\mathrm{it}}$ is the iteration/cycle count, $v_\ell$ the method's own per-cycle visit
count to level $\ell$ (LAMG+: the truncated $\gamma{=}1.5$ schedule; DRA-QC: the K-cycle's
visit doubling with its $n_{\ell+1}>n_\ell^{1/3}$ work cutoff), and $p$ the smoothing-plus-residual
passes per visit (taken equal for both; this if anything undercounts LAMG+'s recombination, so
the near-ties below are conservative for LAMG+). Both solvers run to
$\varepsilon=10^{-8}$ ($\approx 8$ digits), so WPE is directly comparable.

Table~\ref{tab:draqc} shows the comparison is a \emph{clean class split}, not a single winner.
On fast-converging problems DRA-QC's lean hierarchy ($\mathcal C_A\approx1$) makes each cycle
cheap, and it does \emph{less} total work than LAMG+---up to $7\times$ less on an isotropic grid
and $2\times$ less on SPE10, where LAMG+'s richer cycle is more than the problem needs. But on
grid-aligned anisotropy and high-contrast coefficients---families on which DRA-QC was not
evaluated \cite{napov-notay} (its test set is restricted to graphs of ``limited variation
of edge weights'')---degree-based aggregation has no directional information to semicoarsen, so
its iteration count grows sharply ($90\to604$ as $\epsilon$ falls from $10^{-2}$ to $10^{-4}$).
The cheaper cycles do not compensate: DRA-QC performs $9$--$20\times$ \emph{more} work per edge
than LAMG+, whose caliber-2 interpolation (\S\ref{sec:cal2}) holds the convergence factor near
the textbook rate. DRA-QC remains robust in the sense its theory guarantees---a bounded
condition number, hence convergence---but on these families it is robust-but-slow, the regime
LAMG+'s refinements were built for. The two methods are thus genuine peers on
\emph{different} axes: DRA-QC bounds the condition number on real-world graphs and is leaner
there; LAMG+ bounds the asymptotic convergence factor and is an order of magnitude leaner on
grid-aligned anisotropy and high contrast.
