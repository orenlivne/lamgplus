\begin{abstract}
Graph-Laplacian linear systems $L\phi=b$ underlie spectral clustering, centrality and
semi-supervised learning, finite-element analysis, interior-point network-flow solvers,
and electrical-flow computations. We present \textbf{LAMG+}, a lean, parameter-free,
empirically linear-time algebraic multigrid solver for $L\phi=b$: a Julia re-implementation of Lean
Algebraic Multigrid (LAMG) plus
two targeted refinements. We establish three facts: (1) \emph{Fair, same-language
benchmarking} against both variants of approximate-Cholesky (AC), the modern near-linear champion. The two paradigms are \emph{peers with complementary
strengths}---AC is faster on social and citation graphs, LAMG+ on
finite-element/structural matrices, where it is the fastest solver available and the most
memory-frugal; LAMG+ is also $2.2\times$ faster than the worst-case-robust AC variant on large graphs, and solves poorly-separable graphs whose fill
makes sparse Cholesky exhaust memory. (2) \emph{Linear scaling at scale}: LAMG+ is empirically
$\Om$ over the full $1{,}711$-graph SuiteSparse LAMG test set ($100\%$ converged, median $4$ cycles,
log--log slope $1.01$). It was verified for graphs up to $2.4\!\times\!10^8$ nonzeros ($1.2\!\times\!10^8$ edges, \texttt{com-Orkut}), twice larger than the original LAMG study. (3)
\emph{Robustness}: the original MATLAB LAMG was reported non-convergent across the
AC benchmark's test families, whereas our re-derivation converges to their
$10^{-8}$ tolerance on all of them, indicating the gap is specific to that
implementation, not the algorithm. The two refinements, a strength-of-connection aggregation veto and a selective caliber-2 interpolation, close LAMG's grid-aligned-anisotropy outlier tail
(asymptotic convergence factor $\approx0.99\!\to\!0.11$). Both trigger locally and do not substantially increase the complexity. Being a multigrid approach rather than a factorization, LAMG+'s hierarchy is readily reused within a nonlinear network-flow convex equilibrium solver (which in particular solves max-flow) in a companion paper.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
Let $G=(V,E,w)$ be a connected weighted undirected graph with $|V|=n$ nodes, $|E|=m$
edges, and positive weights $w:E\to\R^+$. Its Laplacian $L=D-W$ ($W$ the weighted
adjacency, $D$ the weighted-degree diagonal) is symmetric positive semidefinite with the
constant vector $\mathbf 1$ spanning its null space; equivalently
$\phi^{\top}L\phi=\sum_{(u,v)\in E}w_{uv}(\phi_u-\phi_v)^2$. We solve the compatible
system
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sys}
L\phi=b,\qquad \mathbf 1^{\top}\phi=0,\qquad \mathbf 1^{\top}b=0 .
\end{equation}
Typically $m\ll n^2$ and $L$ is sparse. Our goal is an iterative solver for
\eqref{eq:sys} that uses $\Om$ storage and $\mathcal{O}(m\log(1/\varepsilon))$ operations
for an $\varepsilon$-accurate solution, with \emph{bounded hidden constants on the graphs
that arise in applications}---hundreds, not millions. As in the original LAMG work
\cite{lamg}, we target good \emph{empirical} performance over a diverse test set rather
than provable worst-case complexity on adversarial graphs; in practice the latter bounds
are loose or absent on the hidden constants that govern wall-clock time \cite{spielman-review}.

\subsection{Applications}\label{sec:apps}
System \eqref{eq:sys} is fundamental to many computations \cite[\S2]{spielman-review}:
elliptic PDEs discretised by finite elements on unstructured grids \cite{boman-fem};
interior-point methods for network-flow linear programming, whose inner solve is a
weighted Laplacian \cite{daitch-spielman}; electrical flow through a resistor network;
spectral clustering, graph embedding, and ancestry discovery in machine learning
\cite{ng-jordan-weiss}; and the Fiedler eigenvector, which measures algebraic
connectivity and underlies graph partitioning \cite{fiedler}. Solving $L\phi=b$ is also a
stepping stone to the Laplacian eigenproblem, and---through a smooth nonlinear
reformulation---to network flow itself: a saturating-resistor model recasts max-flow as a
sequence of weighted-Laplacian solves, each handled by LAMG+. We plan to develop that
extension in a companion paper and return to it in \S\ref{sec:conclusion}.

\subsection{Related work}\label{sec:related}
\emph{Direct methods.} A Cholesky factorisation $P^{\top}LP=\hat L\hat L^{\top}$ under a
fill-reducing ordering (minimum-degree, nested dissection) is exact and, with a good
ordering, very fast \cite{davis-cholmod,george-nd}. But fill is governed by separator
quality: $\mathcal{O}(n^{1.5})$ work on planar graphs \cite{lipton-rose-tarjan},
$\mathcal{O}(n^3)$ in general, and---on low-diameter social and citation graphs with no
small separators---a supernodal factorisation that exhausts memory before it finishes.

\emph{Graph-theoretic iterative methods.} Spielman and Teng \cite{spielman-teng} and
successors \cite{kelner-stoc} build spectral sparsifiers as preconditioners, achieving
for any SDD system a provably near-linear $\mathcal{O}(m\,\mathrm{polylog}\,n\cdot\log(1/\varepsilon))$ bound. The hidden constants are large; the fast practical realisation is the
approximate-Cholesky solver \texttt{approxChol} \cite{kelner-stoc,laplacians}, recently made
substantially more robust by Gao, Kyng, and Spielman \cite{gks2023} (the
\texttt{approxchol\_lap2} variant we benchmark), and paralleled by randomized-Cholesky direct
solvers such as RCHOL \cite{rchol}. We benchmark against both \texttt{approxChol} variants as
our strongest near-linear baselines (\S\ref{sec:compete}).

\emph{Algebraic multigrid.} AMG \cite{brandt-amg,ruge-stuben} builds a hierarchy of coarser
operators from matrix entries alone. Classical AMG selects a coarse node subset; aggregation
AMG \cite{vanek-mandel-brezina,notay} groups fine nodes into aggregates. AMG excels on
discretised PDEs but its complexity is hard to control on general graphs: coarse operators
densify on scale-free hubs, and higher-order interpolation makes this worse. A line of work
targets graph Laplacians specifically: Napov and Notay's degree-aware rooted aggregation with
aggregate-quality control \cite{napov-notay}, matching-based multilevel preconditioners
\cite{brannick-match}, and effective-resistance/diffusion-distance coarsening with
least-squares interpolation from relaxed vectors \cite{lee2024}---the last closely related to
LAMG's affinity. Bootstrap AMG \cite{brandt-bootstrap} and algebraic distance
\cite{ron-safro-brandt} likewise learn the coarsening from relaxed test vectors, at higher setup
cost; a recent direction learns the prolongation itself with graph neural networks
\cite{learned-amg}. LAMG+, by contrast, is parameter-free and training-free. Combinatorial
Multigrid (CMG) \cite{cmg} also targets general topologies; CMG and LAMG had comparable average
speed but LAMG was markedly more robust \cite{lamg}. General-purpose aggregation packages
(PyAMG \cite{pyamg}, hypre/BoomerAMG \cite{boomeramg}) are tuned for PDE discretisations rather
than the irregular degree distributions of graph Laplacians. In the broadest recent comparison
\cite{gks2023}, CMG, BoomerAMG, and PETSc all failed to converge across the chimera and SDDM
families, leaving the approximate-elimination solver AC (the \texttt{approxchol\_lap2} variant)
the only blackbox method robust across all of them; the original MATLAB LAMG was reported
non-convergent there, whereas our independent re-derivation converges on those families
(\S\ref{sec:families})---a difference specific to that implementation, not the algorithm. We take
AC as our sole near-linear baseline, with CHOLMOD for the
direct-method crossover.

\subsection{Contribution}\label{sec:contrib}
LAMG \cite{lamg} is an accelerated caliber-1 aggregation AMG that attains $\Om$ on general
graphs---more precisely $\mathcal{O}(m\log(1/\varepsilon))$ for an $\varepsilon$-accurate
solution---by regulating the coarse-operator \emph{energy} rather than its size. We present
\textbf{LAMG+}: an open re-derivation of the algorithm (validated line-by-line against the
original MATLAB reference, App.~\ref{sec:valid}) that we use to establish three things.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=1pt]
\item \emph{A fair, same-language adjudication against the modern near-linear champion}
(\S\ref{sec:compete}). LAMG (2012) predates Spielman's approximate-Cholesky line
\cite{laplacians,gks2023}---now the dominant practical near-linear Laplacian solver---and was never
compared with it. Running both in the same Julia process, we find the two paradigms are
\emph{peers with complementary strengths}: approximate-Cholesky is faster on social and citation
graphs, while on FE/structural matrices LAMG+ is the fastest \emph{and} most
memory-frugal of all three, and $2.2\times$ faster on large graphs than the worst-case-robust
approximate-Cholesky variant. Sparse Cholesky cannot factor the largest poorly-separable matrices.
The split is algorithmic (coarsening vs.\ fill), not a compiler artifact.
\item \emph{Linear scaling at scale} (\S\ref{sec:scaling}). LAMG+ is empirically $\Om$ over
$1{,}711$ SuiteSparse graphs---$100\%$ converged, median $4$ cycles, log--log slope $1.01$---and
builds its multilevel hierarchy on a $2.3\!\times\!10^8$-edge graph, two orders of magnitude beyond
LAMG's original demonstration, with no tuning.
\item \emph{Robustness, closing LAMG's outlier tail} (\S\ref{sec:choices},\,\S\ref{sec:families}).
The original MATLAB LAMG was reported non-convergent across the approximate-Cholesky benchmark's
test families; our independent re-derivation converges to their $10^{-8}$ tolerance on all of them,
indicating the gap is specific to that implementation, not the algorithm.
Two lean, \emph{local} refinements---a strength-of-connection veto and a selective caliber-2
interpolation, both \emph{anticipated as future work} in \cite{lamg}---turn LAMG's stalled
grid-aligned-anisotropy rate ($\acf\approx0.99$) into $\approx0.11$ at $\approx\!1\%$ median
overhead, each acting only on the locally anisotropic nodes that need it. The ideas are LAMG's; the
correct lean realisation and the at-scale evidence are ours.
\item \emph{A forensic account of LAMG's load-bearing design choices} (\S\ref{sec:alg},\,\S\ref{sec:ablation}).
Several of LAMG's setup choices---no aggregate-size cap, orphan nodes left as singleton seeds, a
directional energy guard---are individually \emph{load-bearing} yet undocumented as essential; we
isolate them and quantify, by single-choice ablation, that replacing any one by a plausible correlate
silently destroys convergence on a whole graph class (web ACF $0.002\!\to\!0.97$, for one). Here
reproduction \emph{is} discovery: knowing which heuristics carry the algorithm, and by how much, is
what makes it transferable beyond the original implementation.
\end{itemize}
The open implementation makes all of the above reproducible; LAMG+ needs no parameter tuning.

\section{The LAMG algorithm}\label{sec:alg}
We summarise LAMG \cite{lamg} in enough detail to make this paper self-contained; the
refinements of \S\ref{sec:choices} presuppose it.

\paragraph{Multigrid in one paragraph} A relaxation such as Gauss--Seidel (GS) applied to
$L\phi=b$ removes the high-residual (oscillatory) error in a few sweeps but stalls on the
\emph{algebraically smooth} error---the slow, low-energy modes. Multigrid corrects those
on a coarser graph: it represents the fine smooth error $e\approx Pe^c$ by interpolation
$P\in\R^{n\times n_c}$ from a coarse vector $e^c$, solves the Galerkin coarse system
$L^c e^c=P^{\top}(b-L\tilde\phi)$ with $L^c=P^{\top}LP$, and corrects
$\tilde\phi\leftarrow\tilde\phi+Pe^c$. Applied recursively over $\ell=1,\dots,L$ levels
this is a multigrid cycle; the work is geometric in the per-level coarsening ratio.
LAMG's two coarsening operators---elimination and aggregation---are alternated until the
coarsest level is small.

\paragraph{Relaxation} LAMG relaxes with Gauss--Seidel,
$\phi_u\leftarrow\big(b_u-\sum_{v\neq u}L_{uv}\phi_v\big)/L_{uu}$, an effective smoother
for SPD systems that needs no damping parameter.

\paragraph{Low-degree elimination} Let $F\subseteq V$ be a maximal independent set of
nodes of degree $\le d_{\max}=4$. Because $F$ is independent, $L_{FF}$ is diagonal and the
Schur complement onto $C=V\setminus F$ is \emph{exact}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:elim}
L^c=L_{CC}-L_{CF}L_{FF}^{-1}L_{FC},\qquad
\phi_F=P_F\phi_C+L_{FF}^{-1}b_F,\quad P_F=-L_{FF}^{-1}L_{FC}.
\end{equation}
Eliminating an $F$-node of degree $\le 3$ does not increase $m$; the rare degree-$4$ node
may add an edge. Elimination removes the effectively-one-dimensional part of the graph at
zero approximation cost (up to fill), shrinking $n$ before each aggregation step.

\paragraph{Caliber-1 aggregation} Each remaining node is assigned to exactly one
aggregate, giving a piecewise-constant (``caliber-1'') interpolation with $P_{u,A(u)}=1$.
This keeps $L^c=P^{\top}LP$ \emph{as sparse as the graph}---it cannot manufacture the dense
coarse rows that sink classical AMG on hubs. Aggregates are formed from $K$ \emph{test
vectors} $X=(x^{(1)},\dots,x^{(K)})$, each $\nu$ GS sweeps of $Lx=0$ from a random start:
these are samples of the algebraically smooth subspace the coarse grid must capture, with
no geometry assumed. (LAMG uses $K=4$ at the finest level, raises $K$ by one per coarser
level to a cap of $10$, and smooths each by $\nu=3$ sweeps \cite[\S4.1]{lamg}.) The proximity of an edge $(u,v)$ is the \emph{affinity}, the squared
cosine of the two test-vector profiles \cite[Eq.~3.3]{lamg},
\begin{equation}\label{eq:aff}
c_{uv}=\frac{\big(\sum_k x^{(k)}_u x^{(k)}_v\big)^2}
{\big(\sum_k (x^{(k)}_u)^2\big)\big(\sum_k (x^{(k)}_v)^2\big)}\in[0,1].
\end{equation}
A node $u$ joins the seed $s$ of maximal affinity that is \emph{admissible} under the
\emph{energy-ratio guard}. With the nodal energy
$E_u(x;y)=\tfrac12 L_{uu}y^2-B_u(x)\,y+C_u(x)$, $B_u=\sum_v w_{uv}x_v$,
$C_u=\tfrac12\sum_v w_{uv}x_v^2$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:guard}
q_{u\leftarrow s}=\max_{1\le k\le K}
\frac{E_u\!\big(x^{(k)};x^{(k)}_s\big)}{\min_y E_u\!\big(x^{(k)};y\big)}\le Q,\qquad Q=2.5 .
\end{equation}
The numerator is $u$'s energy when caliber-1 \emph{forces} $u=x_s$; the denominator is its
relaxed (GS-optimal) energy $\min_y E_u=E_u(x;B_u/L_{uu})$. Their ratio is the factor by
which lumping $u$ into $s$'s aggregate inflates the local energy; capping it bounds the
coarse-operator energy aggregate by aggregate, hence the two-level factor
$\rho\lesssim 1-1/Q$ \cite[\S3.4]{lamg}. Crucially the guard regulates \emph{energy, not
size}---there is \emph{no} hard aggregate-size cap: the same $Q$ admits a $100$-node hub
aggregate (neighbours agree on the slow modes, $q\approx1$) yet forbids a $3$-node mesh
aggregate (neighbours disagree, $q$ shoots up). Two companion conventions complete the rule
and are equally load-bearing: a node that finds no admissible seed stays a singleton seed (an
\emph{orphan}), never force-absorbed; and the guard is \emph{directional}---\eqref{eq:guard}
scores $u$ forced onto seed $s$, $q_{u\leftarrow s}$, and is not symmetrised in $(u,s)$. These
are LAMG's choices \cite[\S3.4]{lamg}; substituting a plausible correlate for any of them (a
size cap, forced absorption, a symmetric guard) silently over- or under-coarsens some graph
class, so \S\ref{sec:choices} concerns only where LAMG+ \emph{changes} LAMG. The guard
dominates setup cost and is evaluated in $\Om$ via three bulk-precomputed moments per node
(App.~\ref{sec:guardeval}).

\paragraph{Cycle and acceleration} The solve runs a $\gamma$-cycle whose per-level index
LAMG fixes by a work rule \cite[Eq.~4.1]{lamg}: elimination levels use $\gamma_\ell=1$, and an
aggregation level uses
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gamma}
\gamma_\ell=\begin{cases}1.5, & |E_\ell|>0.1\,|E|\ \ \text{(finer levels)},\\[2pt]
\min\{2,\;0.7/\tau_\ell\}, & \text{otherwise (coarser levels)},\end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $\tau_\ell=|E_{\ell+1}|/|E_\ell|$ is the level-$\ell$ coarsening ratio. The cap bounds
per-cycle work geometrically, guaranteeing $\Om$. The coarse-level branch raises $\gamma_\ell$
toward $2$ where coarsening is aggressive but lowers it toward $1$ where coarsening is weak
($\tau_\ell\to1$); LAMG+ keeps the same rule with a slightly looser work cap (\S\ref{sec:calib}).
After each cycle the finest iterate is improved by a min-residual
(Krylov) recombination over a short history of saved iterates \cite[\S7.8.2]{guide}:
$\alpha=\arg\min_\alpha\|r-LE\alpha\|$ with $E=[\,x_i-x\,]$, $LE=[\,r-r_i\,]$, then
$x\leftarrow x+E\alpha$. A level's history accumulates across the cycle's $\gamma$
revisits; the finest persists across cycles.

\section{Two refinements beyond LAMG}\label{sec:choices}
Reproducing LAMG (\S\ref{sec:alg})---no size cap, orphan singletons, a directional guard, the
cycle-index calibration of \S\ref{sec:calib}---is a prerequisite, not a contribution. LAMG+ departs
from LAMG (Table~\ref{tab:diff}) by (1)~avoiding aggregation across a weak edge, via a
strength-of-connection veto (\S\ref{sec:soc}), and (2)~judiciously raising the interpolation caliber
from $1$ to $2$ (\S\ref{sec:cal2}); both target (local) grid-aligned anisotropy---the outlier tail
LAMG reported and sketched as future work \cite[\S5.2,\,\S6.1]{lamg}. Together they turn a stalled
$\acf\approx0.99$ into $\approx0.11$ on the worst anisotropic grids while leaving every other class
untouched; the ideas are LAMG's own, and our contribution is their correct lean realisation and
at-scale evidence (\S\ref{sec:results}). Both refinements are \emph{local}---the veto a per-edge
test, caliber-2 a per-node test, each firing only where the matrix is locally anisotropic and inert
($\approx\!0\%$ of nodes) elsewhere---so refinement follows where the solver is locally slow, never a
global average: a graph that is mostly isotropic with a small anisotropic region defeats any global
trigger yet is handled correctly, node by node (\S\ref{sec:calib}). Because the two act at different
stages (which nodes aggregate, how they interpolate), they are independent and individually
ablatable (\S\ref{sec:ablation}).

\subsection{A strength-of-connection veto}\label{sec:soc}
Affinity \eqref{eq:aff} ranks a node's neighbours by how well their relaxed test-vector
profiles align, and aggregation merges a node with its highest-affinity admissible neighbour.
On a strongly grid-aligned anisotropic grid the test vectors are nearly flat along the weak
direction, so the $K$-sample affinity of a weak (weight-$\epsilon$) edge is computed from
almost-identical profiles and can, on an unlucky sample, \emph{exceed} that of the genuinely
strong (weight-$1$) edge---we measure $0.97$ versus $0.89$ at one interior node of a $256^2$,
$\epsilon=10^{-4}$ grid. Neither the affinity nor the energy guard $Q$ catches this: both are
computed from the \emph{same} test vectors and are fooled identically. The \emph{matrix} is
not---the conductance ratio there is $10^4$. A single such inversion creates one
cross-weak-direction merge, a localised residual hot-spot (peak-to-mean $>10^3$), and a stalled
rate ($\acf\approx0.99$) that no amount of $\gamma$ or recombination repairs, and that
caliber-2 alone cannot reach past either.

The veto is one line: never aggregate across an edge whose weight is below a fraction
$\tau_{\mathrm{soc}}$ of the node's strongest incident weight,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:soc}
\text{skip edge }(u,v)\ \text{in aggregation if}\quad |w_{uv}|<\tau_{\mathrm{soc}}\,\max_{t\sim u}|w_{ut}|,
\qquad \tau_{\mathrm{soc}}=0.05 .
\end{equation}
It costs $\Oone$ per edge, changes no asymptotics, and is inert wherever a node's incident
weights are comparable. With it, the $256^2$ and $384^2$ grids drop from $\acf\approx0.99$ to
$0.11$ across all sizes, seeds, and $\epsilon\le10^{-1}$ (worst $0.117$ over $24$ cases); on a
$42$-graph random SuiteSparse sample and the social/FE giants to $1.5\times10^7$ edges it is
neutral (worst ACF ratio $1.22$, no regression). The defect is rare---it needs a near-degenerate
weak direction \emph{and} an unlucky sample---which is why stock LAMG mostly avoids it and
reports anisotropy as bounded-but-slow rather than stalled; the veto removes the residual tail
and, crucially, is what lets the caliber-2 fit of \S\ref{sec:cal2} act on a clean aggregation.

\subsection{Selective caliber-2 on one-dimensional chains}\label{sec:cal2}
The veto fixes \emph{which} nodes aggregate; caliber-2 changes \emph{how} they
interpolate---the one place LAMG's strict caliber-1 design leaves a bounded but non-textbook
rate. On grid-aligned anisotropy the affinity correctly semicoarsens along the strong direction
(the aggregates are well placed), but the caliber-1 piecewise-constant interpolation across each
$2{:}1$ aggregate recovers only half of the smooth-in-strong mode, inflating the energy ratio to
$q\approx2$ and pinning the two-level factor at $\rho\approx0.5$ (App.~\ref{sec:aniso}). Where a
fine node's \emph{strong} edges reach exactly two coarse aggregates---a locally one-dimensional
neighbourhood---LAMG+ adds a second, caliber-2 parent with a single fitted weight $w$
(App.~\ref{sec:aniso} derives it). Measured per level with an exact coarse solve (so the
$\gamma$ schedule cannot mask it), the two-level factor on the $256^2$, $\epsilon=10^{-4}$ grid
falls from $0.46$ (caliber-1, veto on) to $0.28$ at every aggregation level; with $\gamma$ and
recombination the full-cycle rate reaches $\approx0.11$. The gate is \emph{self-targeting}:
$\approx\!0\%$ of nodes upgrade on isotropic graphs, so over a $1{,}267$-graph SuiteSparse
sample it rescues the anisotropic minority (\texttt{NACA0015}, \texttt{epb3}, \texttt{darcy}
from $100$ cycles to $5$--$8$; the \texttt{thermomech}/\texttt{thermal} stiffness matrices from
non-convergence to $\approx\!13$) while leaving the easy majority's solve essentially unchanged,
at $\approx\!1\%$ median wall-clock. It is on by default. The two refinements are complementary:
caliber-2 without the veto still stalls at $0.99$ on the worst grids, the veto without caliber-2
reaches only the caliber-1 ceiling $\approx0.46$; together they give $0.11$ (\S\ref{sec:ablation}).

\subsection{A single, local configuration}\label{sec:calib}
LAMG+ runs one configuration on every graph: a cheap $K{=}4$ affinity with both refinements always
on. The interpolation caliber is raised from $1$ to $2$ wherever a node is locally one-dimensional
(\S\ref{sec:cal2}) and left at caliber-1 everywhere else, so the extra cost falls only on the
anisotropic nodes. Four test vectors are an ample sample for the single least-squares weight each
upgraded node fits, so we simply fix $K{=}4$ rather than accumulate test vectors with depth as the
original LAMG does (raising $K$ would pay off only together with more relaxation sweeps $\nu$, which
we do not pursue).

Because the caliber decision is taken per node, it needs no global control---and that is what makes
it robust. A graph that is mostly isotropic with a small anisotropic region is handled correctly:
the upgrade fires exactly on that region. Table~\ref{tab:adaptive} makes the point with a $64^2$
anisotropic patch embedded in a $256^2$ or $512^2$ isotropic grid---only $6\%$ and $1.5\%$ of the
nodes, far too few to trip any graph-averaged trigger---where caliber-1 stalls ($\acf\approx0.3$)
while the always-on local caliber-2 recovers a healthy rate ($\acf\approx0.02$, five cycles). This
is the classical principle that refinement should follow where the solver is \emph{locally} slow,
not a global average \cite[\S9.5]{guide}.

\paragraph{Cycle index (a reproduction calibration, not a refinement)} LAMG's work rule sets
$\gamma_\ell=\min(1.5,\,0.7/\tau_\ell)$, floored at $1$; LAMG+ uses the same rule with a slightly
looser cap and no depth decay,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gammaplus}
\gamma_\ell=\min\!\big(1.5\,g^{\,\ell-2},\;0.95/\tau_\ell,\;3\big),\qquad \gamma_\ell\ge1,\ g=1.
\end{equation}
An intermediate state of our port carried a spurious geometric decay ($g=0.7$) that drove
$\gamma\to1$ by level four regardless of coarsening; that was a bug, removed here. A rare
deep-hierarchy monitor regrows the schedule ($g=1.15$) if the cycle factor exceeds $0.6$; it never
fires on any graph or protocol in this paper and exists for the companion flow solver's
source--sink dipole right-hand sides.

\section{Numerical results}\label{sec:results}
LAMG+ is implemented in Julia, and all reported times are wall-clock, measured on an Apple M5~Pro
(18-core, 48~GB RAM); the competitor solvers of \S\ref{sec:compete} run in the same Julia process
under identical compilation, so the wall-clock comparisons are language-fair.
Throughout this section, every solve uses the right-hand side $b=L\,x_{\mathrm{true}}$ with
$x_{\mathrm{true}}$ a standard-normal random vector projected to zero mean, and runs to relative
residual $\|b-L\,x\|\le10^{-8}\,\|b\|$ unless a different tolerance is stated. This choice of $b$
exercises the full spectrum through a known solution; right-hand sides concentrated on extreme
smooth modes (e.g.\ a far-separated source--sink dipole) arise in the companion flow paper and are
discussed there and in the deep-hierarchy remark of \S\ref{sec:calib}.

\paragraph{Test corpus} All experiments draw from one fixed corpus, the union of three sources:
(i)~the complete $1{,}711$-graph SuiteSparse collection used as the LAMG test set
($100\le n\le1.2\!\times\!10^7$), which drives the scaling study (\S\ref{sec:scaling}) and, restricted
to its $m>10^6$ members, the head-to-head competition (\S\ref{sec:compete});
(ii)~instances from the approximate-Cholesky robustness benchmark \cite{gks2023}, generated by its
own generators---all $30$ \texttt{chimera} and weighted-\texttt{chimera} graphs and all $9$
uniform/anisotropic/high-contrast Poisson grids---plus our synthesized Kyng--Sachdeva classes
(Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi, Barab\'asi--Albert, stochastic-block, $k$-regular, power-law-cluster), used for
robustness (\S\ref{sec:families}); and (iii)~the genomic de Bruijn giant \texttt{kmer\_V1r}
($2.1\!\times\!10^8$ nodes, $2.3\!\times\!10^8$ edges, $6.8\!\times\!10^8$ nonzeros) for setup scale
(\S\ref{sec:scaling}). Every solver runs on the same exported $(L,b)$; all per-graph setup and solve
times are recorded and released with the code so the tables can be regenerated. The largest graph
solved end-to-end is \texttt{com-Orkut} ($1.2\!\times\!10^8$ edges, $2.4\!\times\!10^8$ nonzeros,
$2$ cycles); on \texttt{kmer\_V1r} the hierarchy builds but the solve is memory-bound at $48$\,GB
(\S\ref{sec:scaling}).

\subsection{Linear scaling}\label{sec:scaling}
Figure~\ref{fig:scaling} plots per-edge setup and solve time versus $m$ for all $1{,}711$
SuiteSparse graphs (the LAMG test set: $50\le n\le10^6$), spanning $4.3$
decades ($m$ from $52$ to $9.8\!\times\!10^5$ edges). Every one converges to relative residual
$10^{-8}$, in a median of $4$ cycles. The log--log slopes of per-edge time are $+0.005$ (setup)
and $-0.002$ (solve): per-edge cost is independent of $m$, the empirical $\Om$ statement
(equivalently, total setup$+$solve time scales as $m^{1.01}$). The medians are
$t_{\text{setup}}/m\approx0.24~\mu$s/edge (IQR $[0.18,0.34]$) and
$t_{\text{solve}}/m\approx0.19~\mu$s/edge (IQR $[0.10,0.43]$). The wide vertical scatter is about a
\emph{flat} line---per-instance conditioning (level count, separator quality), not growth with
$m$; the largest per-edge costs are the strongly anisotropic grids of App.~\ref{sec:aniso}. Beyond
the corpus, the construction scales two orders of magnitude further: on a $2.3\!\times\!10^8$-edge
genomic graph (\texttt{kmer\_V1r}, $2.1\!\times\!10^8$ nodes) LAMG+ builds its hierarchy at the
fixed $K{=}4$ default---the same cheap configuration it uses everywhere, with no escalation and no
probe to misfire. This de Bruijn graph is slow by diameter, not anisotropy; a global trigger keyed
on a trial solve would misread that as a stall, whereas the local design never asks the question.
The build is a $21$-level hierarchy of operator complexity $2.51$ in $375$\,s, the setup remaining
$\Om$ two decades past the largest LAMG instance. The iterative solve on that one instance exceeded
our $48$\,GB: at this scale it is memory-bound, not time-bound.

The finding is a clean \emph{class split}, not a single winner. On low-diameter social, web, and

of the three} (geometric-mean $1.76\times$ over the fast \texttt{approxchol\_lap} across the $34$

the near-linear solvers. Beyond the table, LAMG+ solves \texttt{soc-LiveJournal1}

test set~\cite{lamg}.

\paragraph{An a-priori selection rule} The split is not merely descriptive: it is \emph{predictable}
from one cheap statistic, the mean degree $\bar d=2m/n$, known before either solver runs. Over the
$201$ large graphs the rule ``use LAMG+ iff $\bar d>29$'' agrees with the measured $10^{-8}$ winner
$90.5\%$ of the time (and $90.5\%$ at $10^{-4}$, threshold $\bar d>14$); the point-biserial correlation
of $\log\bar d$ with a LAMG+ win is $+0.68$. The mechanism is the one behind the FE/structural win:
approximate-Cholesky's fill grows with elimination-clique size, hence with degree, while caliber-1
aggregation collapses dense neighbourhoods at $\Oone$ cost---so high mean degree favours LAMG+ and low
degree favours sampled elimination. The right solver can thus be chosen from the degree distribution
alone.

\subsection{Robustness across Spielman's test families}\label{sec:families}
The crossover above is a speed result; robustness---converging at all---is a separate axis, and the
one on which graph-Laplacian solvers most often differ. Gao, Kyng, and Spielman \cite{gks2023}
report AC as the only solver in their study (alongside CMG, hypre/BoomerAMG, PETSc, and ICC) to
converge across their full family set---random \texttt{chimera} graphs and randomly-weighted
variants, SDDM ``chimeras'', adversarial ``Sachdeva stars'', an SPE benchmark, and
uniform/anisotropic/high-contrast Poisson grids. That robustness is precisely why we take AC as the
near-linear baseline worth a same-machine comparison.

LAMG+ meets the same bar, on the AC benchmark's full family set, generated by its own generators
(Spielman's \texttt{Laplacians.jl}). With the standard right-hand side ($b=L\,x_{\mathrm{true}}$,
zero-mean), LAMG+ converges to \emph{both} $10^{-8}$ and $10^{-4}$ on every one of the $164$ instances:
\texttt{chimera}s and their weighted, uniform, and semi-weighted variants
($96$ graphs, $n=10^4$ to $10^6$), SDDM chimeras ($12$), $3$-D Poisson grids with uniform,
anisotropic, and high-contrast coefficients ($12$), Sachdeva-style stars ($3$), and---extending
\emph{past} the scale at which AC was benchmarked---$41$ large SuiteSparse graphs up to
\texttt{com-Orkut} ($1.2\times10^8$ edges, $2.4\times10^8$ nonzeros). Of the benchmark's families we
omit only the external SPE fluid dataset; its Flow-IPM matrices are exercised directly by the
companion solver, where LAMG+ is the inner Laplacian solve. Together with the $1{,}711$-graph
scaling set (\S\ref{sec:scaling}), every instance converges, in a median of $4$ cycles. The original
MATLAB LAMG was reported non-convergent on parts of this family set \cite{gks2023}; we did not
reproduce that run and do not diagnose it, observing only that our independent re-derivation
converges on all of these families---evidence that the reported non-convergence was specific to that
implementation or its invocation, not to the algorithm. Establishing this required matching the
original's design choices (\S\ref{sec:alg}) line by line.

On these well-structured families AC is the faster solver: chimeras and grids have the bounded
degrees and separators its sampled elimination exploits, and LAMG+'s lean-aggregation setup is not
amortised over a $3$--$6$ cycle solve. LAMG+'s speed advantage is the large poorly-separable class
of Table~\ref{tab:compete}. The point across the families is that \emph{both} solvers are robust, so
the comparison is one of speed---which we report next over the full corpus, in time per nonzero, the
worst-case-oriented metric of \cite{gks2023}.

\subsection{Lower accuracy shifts the balance toward LAMG+}\label{sec:tol4}
Many applications need only a few digits. At relative residual $10^{-4}$ the LAMG+ solve collapses
to a \emph{single} cycle, so the solve's share of the total shrinks and setup---the phase where
LAMG+'s cheap fixed-$K{=}4$ build is strongest---dominates. Lowering the tolerance therefore
amplifies whichever solver wins on setup, and that helps LAMG+ across the board (Table~\ref{tab:compete},
bottom block). Against the fast \texttt{approxchol\_lap} the aggregate geometric mean rises from
$0.99\times$ at $10^{-8}$ (parity) to $1.20\times$ at $10^{-4}$---LAMG+ ahead---and its win rate
over the $201$ large graphs rises from $56\%$ to $64\%$. Against the robust AC the lead widens from
$2.20\times$ to $2.91\times$ ($76\%\!\to\!84\%$ of head-to-heads). The shift is largest exactly
where LAMG+ already leads, the FE/structural class, since there the solve was already short and
setup already dominant. (Reusing one hierarchy across many right-hand sides does \emph{not} add a
further LAMG+ advantage here: both solvers amortise their one-time setup over repeated solves, so the
many-RHS comparison reduces to solve cost alone, where the cheaper-per-solve solver---often
\texttt{approxChol} on these graphs---wins.)

\subsection{Anisotropy: the veto and the caliber-2 extension}\label{sec:aniso-results}
Grid-aligned anisotropy is baseline (caliber-1) LAMG+'s worst class. On a $64\times64$ grid with
strong weight $1$ and weak weight $\epsilon$ the full solver converges at a bounded but
non-textbook $\acf\approx0.27$--$0.39$ for $\epsilon\le10^{-1}$ (vs.\ $0.01$ isotropic); the
caliber-2 gate (\S\ref{sec:cal2}) recovers a near-geometric rate, $\acf=0.007/0.05/0.10$ at
$\epsilon=10^{-1}/10^{-2}/10^{-4}$, at \emph{lower} operator complexity (sharper interpolation
buys fewer levels). On \emph{larger} grids ($256^2$, $384^2$) a second, rarer failure appears:
the affinity inversion of \S\ref{sec:soc} produces a single bad merge that stalls even caliber-2
at $\acf\approx0.99$. The strength-of-connection veto removes it, and the two refinements together
hold $\acf\approx0.11$ across all sizes, seeds, and $\epsilon\le10^{-1}$ (worst $0.117$ over $24$
cases). On the SuiteSparse stiffness matrices the pair converts $100$-cycle near-failures into
$5$--$14$ cycle solves. This does \emph{not} make LAMG+ the fastest solver on this class---these
matrices are small and well-separated, so \texttt{approxChol} and CHOLMOD stay ahead---so the
value here is robustness, closing LAMG's documented outlier tail, not a new crossover. The
mechanism, the per-level two-level factors, and the weight derivation are in App.~\ref{sec:aniso}.

\section{Toward a parallel LAMG+}\label{sec:parallel}
The results above are single-threaded. Both hot phases parallelize in principle---the setup
(aggregation, test-vector relaxation, Galerkin triple products) and the cycle reduce to the scalable
sparse primitives (sparse matrix--vector products, maximal independent set, segmented reductions) that
Bell, Dalton, and Olson \cite{bell-dalton-olson} exposed for GPU AMG and that scale BoomerAMG to
$10^5$ cores \cite{baker-scaling,amgx}---but the one phase that \emph{looks} inherently sequential is
the Gauss--Seidel smoother, since each update reads its already-updated neighbours. We quantify that
it is not, then state the obstacle that remains open.

\paragraph{The smoother survives parallelization (a smoothing-factor analysis)} The standard parallel
substitute for lexicographic GS is \emph{block-Jacobi GS}: partition the nodes into $P$ contiguous
blocks, each of width $W{=}n/P$, of a bandwidth-reducing reverse Cuthill--McKee (RCM)
ordering~\cite{cuthill-mckee}; run serial GS within each block and freeze (Jacobi) the cross-block
coupling, so the blocks relax concurrently. In the RCM order write $A=D-L-L^{\top}$; serial GS uses the
preconditioner $M_{\mathrm{GS}}=D-L$, and block-Jacobi GS its block-diagonal restriction
$M_{\mathrm{BJ}}=D-L_{\mathrm{blk}}$. A relaxation smooths iff it contracts the high-frequency error;
the smoothing factor is $\mu=\|P_{\mathrm{HF}}\,S\,P_{\mathrm{HF}}\|_2$, where $S=I-M^{-1}A$ and
$P_{\mathrm{HF}}$ is the orthogonal projector onto the eigenvectors of $A$ above its median eigenvalue
(on a regular grid, the upper Fourier quadrant). The reference values are $\mu{=}1$ (undamped Jacobi,
no smoothing), $\mu_{\mathrm{GS}}\!\approx\!\tfrac12$, and $\mu_{\mathrm{RB}}{=}\tfrac14$ (red--black).

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:bj}
Let $\sigma=\big(\textstyle\sum_{\mathrm{severed}}w_{uv}\big)\big/\big(\sum_{(u,v)\in E}w_{uv}\big)$ be
the fraction of edge weight cut by the block boundaries. Then $\mu_{\mathrm{BJ}}=\mu_{\mathrm{GS}}+O(\sigma)$,
and for width-$W$ blocks of a $d$-dimensional grid $\sigma=O(1/W)$. Hence $\mu_{\mathrm{BJ}}\to\mu_{\mathrm{GS}}$
as $W\to\infty$, reaching the Jacobi value $\mu{=}1$ only at $W{=}1$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$E:=M_{\mathrm{GS}}-M_{\mathrm{BJ}}$ collects exactly the severed strict-lower couplings, so
$\|E\|=O(\sigma\|A\|)$. Then $S_{\mathrm{BJ}}-S_{\mathrm{GS}}=(M_{\mathrm{GS}}^{-1}-M_{\mathrm{BJ}}^{-1})A
=-\,M_{\mathrm{GS}}^{-1}E\,M_{\mathrm{BJ}}^{-1}A$, whose prefactors are bounded on the high-frequency
subspace, so $\|P_{\mathrm{HF}}(S_{\mathrm{BJ}}-S_{\mathrm{GS}})P_{\mathrm{HF}}\|=O(\sigma)$ and the
estimate follows. On a grid of side $L$ cut into $P{=}L/W$ slabs, the $P{-}1$ interfaces carry
$O(L^{d-1})$ edges each of $O(L^d)$ total, whence $\sigma=O(P/L)=O(1/W)$.
\end{proof}

Three independent measurements---the operator norm, local Fourier analysis (grids only), and direct
high-frequency sweeps---agree where each applies and bear out Proposition~\ref{prop:bj}: at the operative
$P{=}8$, $\mu_{\mathrm{BJ}}$ matches $\mu_{\mathrm{GS}}$ to within a few percent on grids and social graphs
alike, reaching the Jacobi limit only as $W\to1$ (Table~\ref{tab:smooth}). The estimate also predicts the
non-obvious case: as the penalty scales with severed \emph{weight}, an RCM-banded mesh (few but heavy
interface edges) degrades more than a scale-free graph (many but light), as the table shows.

The smoothing factor governs the smoother alone. A block-Jacobi \emph{cycle} can still converge more
slowly with $\mu$ unchanged: the frozen seams act as $\omega{=}1$ Jacobi in the inter-block transfer and
degrade the coarse-grid correction---a separate effect, invisible to $\mu$ and to a cycle count, and
absent from a multicolour smoother that recovers rather than freezes the coupling. Brandt's prescription
is accordingly to \emph{colour, not freeze} (red--black/zebra GS are fully parallel and smooth better,
$\mu{=}\tfrac14$, at lower cost~\cite{guide}). Proposition~\ref{prop:bj} makes the narrower point that
suffices here: the LAMG+ smoother has no \emph{intrinsic} serial bottleneck---its parallel form loses no
smoothing power to leading order---unlike the global triangular solve of an approximate-Cholesky factor
(\S\ref{sec:compete}), whose elimination order has no comparably local form.

\paragraph{The open obstacle is distributed coarsening} An irregular, low-diameter Laplacian must be
partitioned to minimise inter-processor communication---itself a multilevel graph-partitioning problem
\cite{metis}---and aggregates straddling a boundary need a halo exchange per cycle. Whether affinity
aggregation and low-degree elimination keep their lean operator complexity under a
communication-minimising partition (the local, per-node caliber-2 test needs only a node's immediate
neighbourhood, so it distributes naturally) is a project in its own right and beyond this paper's
scope. We note only that nothing in the algorithm is inherently sequential---a no-fill aggregation
hierarchy is, if anything, friendlier to distribution than a sparsified Cholesky factor whose
elimination order is global.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
LAMG+ is a lean, parameter-free, empirically linear-time graph-Laplacian solver. Its contribution
is threefold. \emph{Robustness:} a faithful reproduction of LAMG's load-bearing setup choices
(\S\ref{sec:alg}) plus two targeted, \emph{local} refinements (\S\ref{sec:choices})---a
strength-of-connection veto and a selective caliber-2 interpolation---make one algorithm
converge to $10^{-8}$ across structured, web, social, citation, collaboration, road, and
grid-aligned anisotropic graphs with no per-graph tuning, and on every family of Spielman's
approximate-Cholesky benchmark \cite{gks2023}, including those on which the original MATLAB LAMG was
reported non-convergent. \emph{Scale:} empirical $\Om$ over $1{,}711$ SuiteSparse graphs ($100\%$
converged, median $4$ cycles) and an $\Om$ hierarchy built on a $2.3\!\times\!10^8$-edge graph.
\emph{A fair adjudication of two paradigms:} run in the same language, energy-corrected aggregation
and approximate-Cholesky are peers with complementary strengths---approximate-Cholesky faster on
social and citation graphs, LAMG+ the fastest \emph{and} most memory-frugal solver on
finite-element/structural matrices, and $2.2\times$ faster on large graphs than the
worst-case-robust approximate-Cholesky variant---while sparse Cholesky cannot factor the
largest poorly-separable matrices. Both refinements are local and self-targeting, which is what lets
LAMG+ stay cheap by default---a single fixed configuration, with the anisotropy fix applied node by
node---yet robust on demand; a full distributed parallelization (\S\ref{sec:parallel}) is open---we
establish only that the smoother, often a multigrid solver's sequential bottleneck, admits a parallel
block-Jacobi ordering that preserves its smoothing factor, whereas a sparsified-Cholesky factor's
global elimination order has no comparably local form. Being
a multigrid rather than a factorization, LAMG+'s caliber-1 aggregation, fractional cycle, and
recombination also reuse within a Full-Approximation-Scheme solver~\cite{brandt-guide} for the
nonlinear (max-flow) companion paper.

On a five-point grid with strong (horizontal) weight $1$ and weak (vertical) weight $\epsilon$,
the headline inverts the naive expectation: an isotropic grid is easy ($\acf=0.01$),
\emph{heterogeneous} weights (each edge an independent random factor over three decades) are
also easy ($\acf=0.006$), but \emph{grid-aligned} anisotropy is the hard case
($\acf\approx0.34$--$0.39$ at $\epsilon\le10^{-2}$). The mechanism and the cure are both in the
LAMG design. Affinity \emph{does} semicoarsen---it merges along the strong direction, the right
\emph{kind} of coarsening. The difficulty is neither the smoother nor the coarsening direction
but the caliber-1 piecewise-constant interpolation: it recovers about half the magnitude of a
smooth-in-strong mode, inflating the coarse-operator energy by $q\approx2$ and pinning the
\emph{two-level} factor at $\rho\approx1-1/q\approx0.5$ \cite[\S3.4.3]{lamg}. This is an
interpolation-\emph{order} property, governed by the classical rule that the interpolation order
plus the residual-transfer order must reach the order of the operator, $m_i+m_j\ge m_{ij}$
\cite[\S4.3]{guide}: caliber-1 reproduces constants but not the linear-in-strong mode, so it is
order-deficient for the $m_{ij}{=}2$ Laplacian along the strong chain. The scalar $q$ is the
\emph{symptom}; the cause is order, and the number of coarse parents needed to restore it---the
\emph{caliber}---equals the dimension of the locally-smooth subspace \cite[\S3.1.2]{lamg}. A
strong-direction line smoother would only double-cover the mode the semicoarsening already handles,
and larger aggregates do not help, since $\rho$ is set by $q$, not size.

The order deficit recurs identically on every semicoarsened level, so left uncorrected it
\emph{compounds}: a flat V-cycle ($\gamma=1$, no recombination) sits at $\acf\approx0.77$---the
multilevel rate the deficit accumulates to, not the two-level $\approx0.5$. The fractional cycle
index $\gamma=1.5$ and iterate recombination at every level \cite[\S3.4.5]{lamg} \emph{bound} this
compounding (Table~\ref{tab:aniso}): with both on, the $\epsilon=10^{-4}$ grid converges at
$\acf=0.39$ ($15$ cycles), and recombination off leaves the stationary $0.51$. But recombination
minimises only over the span of saved smoothed iterates; it cannot drive an order-deficient
interpolation to a geometric rate, because the same deficiency reappears at each level. Only raising
the interpolation order (caliber-2) removes it. So at caliber-1 aligned anisotropy is robust but not
textbook---a bounded $\approx0.4$, the correct lean-algebraic trade.

\paragraph{The affinity-inversion defect and the strength-of-connection veto}
Table~\ref{tab:aniso} is a $64^2$ grid, small enough that aggregation never misfires. On larger
grids ($256^2$, $384^2$) a rarer failure surfaces, and it must be fixed before caliber-2 can act.
The affinity \eqref{eq:aff} is a $K$-sample squared cosine of relaxed test-vector profiles; along
the \emph{weak} direction those profiles are nearly identical, so the weak edge's sampled affinity
has high variance and can, at an unlucky interior node, \emph{exceed} the strong edge's---we
measure $0.97$ (weak, weight $\epsilon=10^{-4}$) against $0.89$ (strong, weight $1$) at one node.
The energy guard $Q$ shares the same test vectors and is fooled identically; only the matrix, with
its $10^4$ conductance ratio, is not. The resulting single cross-weak merge is a localised
defect---one size-$4$ aggregate spanning the weak direction amid a sea of correct $2{:}1$
strong-direction pairs---a weakly-connected aggregate with a \emph{very large} local energy ratio
\cite[\S3.4.2]{lamg}. It is the \emph{same} law $\rho\approx1-1/q$ that gives the caliber-1 ceiling,
but now at $q\gg1$ rather than $q\approx2$: one bad aggregate pins a residual hot-spot (peak-to-mean
$>10^3$) and a stalled $\acf\approx0.99$ that neither $\gamma$ nor caliber-2 can pass---and
recombination cannot either, since the defective coarse operator reintroduces the bad mode each
cycle, outside the smoothed-iterate span recombination acts on. The two regimes of one law thus take
two cures: the veto for $q\gg1$, caliber-2 for $q\approx2$. The veto \eqref{eq:soc} removes it
at $\Oone$ per edge by refusing any merge across an edge below $\tau_{\mathrm{soc}}=0.05$ times the
node's strongest incident weight. With the veto on, the per-level two-level factor (exact coarse
solve, $\gamma=1$, so the cycle schedule cannot mask the interpolation) is $\approx0.46$ at
caliber-1 and $\approx0.28$ at caliber-2 across every aggregation level of the $256^2$ grid; the
full solver then reaches $\acf\approx0.11$. The two refinements are thus sequential: the veto
restores a clean semicoarsening, on which the caliber-2 fit below is well posed.

\paragraph{Fitting the caliber-2 weight} A fine node $u$ whose strong edges reach exactly two
coarse aggregates $a,b$ is interpolated by $\phi_u=w\,\phi_a+(1-w)\,\phi_b$; fixing the weights
to sum to $1$ keeps constants exact. The single weight $w$ is fit by least squares over the
\emph{same} $K$ test vectors used for affinity (no extra setup): minimise
$\sum_k \omega_k\big(x^{(k)}_u-[w\,x^{(k)}_a+(1-w)\,x^{(k)}_b]\big)^2$, whose closed form, with
$d_k=x^{(k)}_a-x^{(k)}_b$ and $e_k=x^{(k)}_u-x^{(k)}_b$, is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:wfit}
w^\star=\frac{\sum_k \omega_k\,d_k\,e_k}{\sum_k \omega_k\,d_k^2},
\end{equation}
the regression of $u$'s profile onto the strong chain's endpoints. The smoothness weights
$\omega_k$ down-weight test vectors with large residual energy at $u$. We guard $w^\star\in[0,1]$
(a convex combination, so the Galerkin operator gains no spurious anti-coupling), snapping
near-boundary values to $0/1$; an out-of-range fit signals that $u$ is not truly one-dimensional
and is left caliber-1. The gate fires only when the two strong-reachable aggregates are distinct
and the fit is admissible---self-targeting at no parameter cost. A seed-injection restriction
$R$ with $R\cdot P=I$ (each coarse node injects from its seed) makes the operator Full-Approximation-Scheme-safe,
carrying the extension to the nonlinear companion solver.

\paragraph{The role of caliber-2} The guard $Q$ already makes the solver robust in every
dimension ($\rho\lesssim1-1/Q$, never diverges). What it does not do is make $q$ \emph{small}
where anisotropy is persistent: grid-aligned anisotropy and $1$-D FE chains sit at the ceiling
$q\approx2$ at every level. Caliber-2 attacks that residue, and only where two conditions
coincide: the bound is tight (the strong coupling is a line, so caliber-1 across it is exactly
the half-recovered mode) and the cure is cheap (two collinear parents). Both hold iff a node is
locally one-dimensional. Hence anisotropic $2$-D and $3$-D \emph{rods} (a single strong
direction) become textbook, while $3$-D \emph{plates} (strong coupling in a plane) are locally
two-dimensional: the gate sees $>2$ strong aggregates and correctly declines, since recovering
textbook there needs caliber-3 (three non-collinear parents), unconstructible in the lean design
and left to the guard. The division of labour is clean: the guard buys universal robustness;
caliber-2 buys textbook efficiency in the one regime where the bound is tight and the fix costs
a single parent.

\section{Linear-time evaluation of the energy guard}\label{sec:guardeval}
A direct evaluation of \eqref{eq:guard} is superlinear: recomputing, for each candidate
$(u,s)$ pair, the nodal energy by summing over $u$'s incident edges for each of $K$ test
vectors costs $\mathcal{O}(K\deg u)$ per pair, and a node with $D$ admissible neighbours is
tested $\mathcal{O}(D)$ times, so the work is $\mathcal{O}(K\deg^2)$---concentrated on the
high-degree hubs that dominate the total. The guard admits an $\mathcal{O}(K)$-per-candidate
form. In one $\mathcal{O}(Km)$ pass accumulate, per node $u$, the three moments
$s_0=\sum_v w_{uv}$, $s_1^{(k)}=\sum_v w_{uv}x^{(k)}_v$, $s_2^{(k)}=\sum_v w_{uv}(x^{(k)}_v)^2$.
Then both energies in \eqref{eq:guard} are closed forms: the relaxed (optimal) energy is
$\min_y E_u=\tfrac12\big(s_2^{(k)}-(s_1^{(k)})^2/s_0\big)$ and the forced energy is
$E_u(\cdot;x_s)=\tfrac12\big(x_s^2 s_0-2x_s s_1^{(k)}+s_2^{(k)}\big)$, so each candidate costs
$\mathcal{O}(K)$, independent of degree. Only the \emph{cost} changes, not the value: every
aggregation decision, hierarchy, and ACF is identical to a naive evaluation. This holds the
setup at the flat per-edge cost of Fig.~\ref{fig:scaling} (regression slope $0.008$).

\section{Validation methodology}\label{sec:valid}
The implementation was validated line-by-line against the unmodified MATLAB reference
\texttt{lamg-2.2.1} run under Octave: we recompiled its C MEX kernels (so Octave executes the
\emph{exact} Gauss--Seidel, Galerkin, aggregation-sweep, and low-degree-sweep kernels) and
mechanically adapted Octave's class incompatibilities. We exported each $(L,b)$ from the implementation and loaded the identical
system in Octave, eliminating preprocessing as a variable; both emit a state trace (level,
residual norm, iterate norm) in the same format. We verified that the elimination set $|F|$,
the Schur operators (residual $10^{-16}$), and cycle stationarity (recombination off
$\Rightarrow$ constant asymptotic factor) match, and that the reference performs no weight
preprocessing.
